Frog Egg Cell Model

Figure 1: African Clawed Frog, Xenopus laevis

DataBase Center for Life Science (DBCLS), CC BY 4.0, via Wikimedia Commons

African frog (Xenopus laevis) eggs are a convenient subject for cell division studies.

  • The original egg cell is large enough to observe with the naked eye.
  • Divisions occur every thirty minutes.
  • The cells do not grow; there is no G1 or G2 phase.
  • Frogs are relatively closely related to humans.
Frog eggs usually need to be fertilized to divide, but scientists can chemically induce division.

Figure 2: Xenopus Laevis eggs

Selles, J., Penrad-Mobayed, M., Guillaume, C., Fuger A., Auvray L., Faklaris O., Montel F., CC BY 4.0 , via Wikimedia Commons

Bouncing Frog Eggs

In 1980, Marc Kirschner removed the nucleus of an activated frog egg. The cell 'bounced'- expanding during mitosis, and contracting during interphase.

Split Eggs

In later experiments, scientists physically split an activated egg. The half that contained the nucleus divided as normal. The half without the nucleus expanded and contracted in sync with the other half's division. This led to the discovery of M-phase Promoting Factor (MPF), a protein that triggers mitosis.
